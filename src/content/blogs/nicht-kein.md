# Nicht vs. Kein: The German Negation Guide

Negation looks tiny, but it changes everything.

In English, you often use **not** for almost everything:

* I am **not** tired.
* I do **not** have time.
* That is **not** a problem.

German splits this job between two words:

* **nicht** = not
* **kein** = not a / no / not any

At first, they feel interchangeable. But they are not. Once you understand what each word is actually negating, the choice becomes much easier.

---

## 1. The Core Difference

Use **kein** to negate a noun that has no definite article.

Use **nicht** to negate verbs, adjectives, adverbs, prepositional phrases, definite nouns, names, and whole ideas.

| You want to negate... | Use | Example |
|-----------------------|-----|---------|
| an indefinite noun | **kein** | Ich habe **kein** Auto. |
| a noun with no article | **kein** | Ich trinke **keinen** Kaffee. |
| a verb/action | **nicht** | Ich komme **nicht**. |
| an adjective | **nicht** | Das ist **nicht** teuer. |
| a definite noun | **nicht** | Ich kenne den Mann **nicht**. |
| a prepositional phrase | **nicht** | Wir wohnen **nicht** in Berlin. |

The quick beginner rule:

> If you could say "not a" or "no" before a noun, German probably wants **kein**.

---

## 2. Kein Negates Nouns

**Kein** behaves like the article **ein**. That means it changes depending on gender, number, and case.

| Gender / Number | Nominativ | Akkusativ |
|-----------------|-----------|-----------|
| masculine | kein Mann | keinen Mann |
| feminine | keine Frau | keine Frau |
| neuter | kein Kind | kein Kind |
| plural | keine Kinder | keine Kinder |

Examples:

* *Das ist **kein** Fehler.*  
  That is not a mistake.

* *Ich habe **keinen** Bruder.*  
  I do not have a brother.

* *Sie kauft **keine** Tasche.*  
  She is not buying a bag.

* *Wir haben **keine** Fragen.*  
  We have no questions.

Notice the logic: German is not just saying "not". It is saying "not one / no".

---

## 3. Kein Also Works with Plural Nouns

In English, you often say "no" with plural nouns:

* no children
* no problems
* no questions

German uses **keine**:

* *Ich habe **keine** Kinder.*  
  I do not have children.

* *Es gibt **keine** Probleme.*  
  There are no problems.

* *Wir haben **keine** Tickets.*  
  We do not have tickets.

This is one of the most common uses of **kein**.

---

## 4. Nicht Negates Verbs and Actions

Use **nicht** when the main thing you are negating is the action.

* *Ich komme **nicht**.*  
  I am not coming.

* *Er arbeitet heute **nicht**.*  
  He is not working today.

* *Wir schlafen **nicht**.*  
  We are not sleeping.

Here, there is no noun like "a car" or "a ticket" to negate. You are negating the whole action.

---

## 5. Nicht Negates Adjectives and Adverbs

If you negate a description, use **nicht**.

| Positive | Negative |
|----------|----------|
| Das ist teuer. | Das ist **nicht** teuer. |
| Sie ist müde. | Sie ist **nicht** müde. |
| Er fährt schnell. | Er fährt **nicht** schnell. |
| Das klingt gut. | Das klingt **nicht** gut. |

Common learner mistake:

* Wrong: *Das ist kein teuer.*
* Correct: *Das ist **nicht teuer**.*

Why? Because **teuer** is an adjective, not a noun.

---

## 6. Nicht with Definite Articles

Use **nicht** when the noun has a definite article like **der, die, das, den, dem**.

* *Ich kenne **den** Lehrer **nicht**.*  
  I do not know the teacher.

* *Sie findet **die** Tasche **nicht**.*  
  She cannot find the bag.

* *Wir kaufen **das** Auto **nicht**.*  
  We are not buying the car.

Compare:

| Sentence | Meaning |
|----------|---------|
| Ich habe **kein** Auto. | I do not have a car. |
| Ich kaufe **das** Auto **nicht**. | I am not buying the car. |

**Kein** negates the existence of an indefinite thing.  
**Nicht** negates what happens with a specific thing.

---

## 7. Where Does Nicht Go?

The position of **nicht** depends on what it negates.

### At the end for simple verb negation

* *Ich komme **nicht**.*
* *Sie raucht **nicht**.*
* *Wir verstehen **nicht**.*

### Before adjectives and adverbs

* *Das ist **nicht** einfach.*
* *Er fährt **nicht** schnell.*
* *Die Wohnung ist **nicht** groß.*

### Before prepositional phrases

* *Ich wohne **nicht** in München.*
* *Wir fahren **nicht** mit dem Zug.*
* *Sie wartet **nicht** auf mich.*

This last rule is very useful: when a phrase starts with a preposition, **nicht** often stands directly before it.

---

## 8. Nicht with Separable Verbs and Modal Verbs

With separable verbs, **nicht** usually comes before the separated prefix at the end.

* *Ich rufe dich heute **nicht** an.*  
  I am not calling you today.

* *Sie steht morgen **nicht** früh auf.*  
  She is not getting up early tomorrow.

With modal verbs, **nicht** usually comes before the final infinitive.

* *Ich kann heute **nicht** kommen.*  
  I cannot come today.

* *Wir müssen das **nicht** machen.*  
  We do not have to do that.

This connects nicely to German word order: the final verb area is very important, and **nicht** often appears right before it.

---

## 9. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Better | Why |
|---------|--------|-----|
| Ich habe nicht Auto. | Ich habe **kein** Auto. | indefinite noun |
| Das ist kein gut. | Das ist **nicht** gut. | adjective |
| Ich kenne kein den Mann. | Ich kenne den Mann **nicht**. | definite noun |
| Wir gehen nicht ins Kino? | Correct if negating the destination/action. | **nicht** before prepositional phrase is normal |
| Ich habe keine Zeit nicht. | Ich habe **keine** Zeit. | do not double-negate |

German normally uses one clear negation. Do not add **nicht** after **kein** unless you are creating a very special contrast.

---

## 10. Decision Checklist

Ask yourself these questions:

1. Am I negating a noun with **ein/eine** or no article?  
   -> Use **kein**.

2. Am I negating a plural noun with no article?  
   -> Use **keine**.

3. Am I negating an adjective, verb, adverb, definite noun, name, or prepositional phrase?  
   -> Use **nicht**.

4. Does the sentence already have **der/die/das**?  
   -> Usually use **nicht**, not **kein**.

---

## Summary

* **Kein** negates indefinite nouns: *kein Auto, keine Zeit, keine Fragen*.
* **Nicht** negates actions, descriptions, specific nouns, and phrases.
* **Kein** changes like an article.
* **Nicht** usually stands near the thing it negates.
* Do not translate English "not" mechanically. First ask: "What exactly am I negating?"
